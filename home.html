Happy Accidents Paint Shop

A Bob Ross-themed React App

Background

It's 2020 – life is stressful. Many people I know are going through a hard time. To cheer myself and others up, I decided to create a React app where users can do a relaxing, low-stress activity – browse and shop for paints. To make it as absolutely chill as possible, I decided to theme my paint shop based on Bob Ross's The Joy of Painting. The shop name is based on a well-known quotation of his: "We don't make mistakes, just happy accidents!"

The aesthetic

Bob Ross's aesthetic is anti-perfectionist, perhaps a little kitsch. As a result, I decided to incorporate very literal aesthetic elements from the show using textured backgrounds – the three materials I chose are canvas, cloth, and wood. These represent various painting and palette surfaces. Together, they create a collage-like effect, which amplifies the element of kitsch while still being playful.

Textured "materials" used in the app.

In addition, I decided to add other playful elements to the UI – for instance, calling the cart "Your Palette", allowing users to add or remove one "squeeze of paint" rather than a bottle or other more conventional quantity, and adding backgrounds to the selected filtering buttons that reflected their functionality:

An example of filter backgrounds in the app.

Goal

To give users a pleasant, relaxing paint shopping experience through good usability and accessibility practices.

Usability principles

I took into account a number of usability principles in designing this app. Here are just a few:

Learnability

The app is structured entirely on one page. When the page loads, elements on the bottom are cut off, meaning that scrollability is clear.

Each section (such as filters, or the palette) has clear labels. Different sections – such as the cart – are differentiated using various backgrounds, to help users easily understand the app structure.

To help users figure out which elements are clickable, I added a light drop shadow to all buttons or button groups.

On interaction, filter buttons' backgrounds change color based on the colors they filter (see image above!). This change adds usability "magic" and gives the user immediate feedback on what each button does.

Memorability

Many of the same elements which make an app learnable, such as a clear structure and direct feedback, make it more memorable as well. The usability "magic" of certain buttons, and the playful labeling in "palette" and "adding a squeeze," help the functionality of the app stick in users' minds better.

Accessibility

I checked my app's accessibility using WAVE's accessibility checking extension. There are no errors – meaning that contrast is good, and layout is accessible to screen reading software. In addition, tab order corresponds directly to the app structure. Finally, the ability to undo actions – for example, switching filters back to "all" or removing elements from the cart – make the application more accessible overall!
